Tactics of Chess

4 important tactics every beginner should know

What are tactics?

In chess, tactics are short combinations that help you win material or create checkmate threats. They usually work because the opponent makes a mistake.

A tactic often appears when a piece is not protected or when the opponent’s king is poorly defended.

Beginners do not need to know many tactics. Learning just a few basic ideas already makes a big difference.

Tip: Always look for checks, captures, and threats. These often lead to tactics.

1. Fork

A fork is a move that attacks two or more pieces at the same time. The opponent usually cannot save all attacked pieces.

Forks are very common with the knight, because its movement is hard to predict. However, any piece can create a fork.

Forks are especially strong when one of the attacked pieces is the king, because the king must respond to check.

  • Best targets: king + queen or rook
  • Always check if your forking piece is protected

2. Pin

A pin happens when a piece cannot move without exposing a more valuable piece behind it.

The strongest pin is when a piece is pinned to the king. This piece usually cannot move at all.

Even though the pinned piece remains on the board, it becomes much less useful.

  • Common pin pieces: bishops and rooks
  • Pins are great for building pressure

3. Skewer

A skewer is similar to a pin, but the stronger piece is attacked first. When it moves away, the weaker piece behind it is lost.

Skewers are most effective on open lines, such as files and diagonals.

This tactic often appears later in the game when the board is less crowded.

Simple idea: Force a valuable piece to move, then win the piece behind it.

4. Discovered Attack

A discovered attack happens when one piece moves away and reveals an attack from a piece behind it.

A very strong version is a discovered check, because the opponent must respond to the check immediately.

Discovered attacks are powerful because they create two threats at once.

  • Common with rooks, bishops, and the queen
  • Best when the moved piece also creates a threat

Quick Training Tips

To improve at tactics, it is better to train a little every day instead of playing very long sessions.

Over time, tactical patterns become familiar and easier to spot.

  • Solve 5–10 puzzles per day
  • Ask: Is anything hanging?
  • Look for checks, captures, threats
With practice, tactics will start appearing naturally in your games.
