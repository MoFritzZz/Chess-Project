The Goal of Chess

Chess is a two-player strategy game where each player tries to outthink the other. The main goal is to checkmate the opponent’s king.

Checkmate means that the king is under attack and there is no legal move to escape. The king cannot move away, the attack cannot be blocked, and the attacking piece cannot be captured.

The king is never actually captured in chess. As soon as checkmate is forced, the game ends immediately and the attacking player wins.

During the game, players often put the king in check. This is a warning that the king is in danger and must be protected on the next move.

Tip: If your king is in check, you must react immediately. You can move the king, block the attack, or capture the attacking piece.

The Chessboard

Chess is played on a square 8×8 board with a total of 64 squares. The squares alternate between light and dark colors.

A simple but important setup rule is that the bottom-right square must always be light. If the board is placed incorrectly, the game setup will be wrong.

Each player starts on their own side of the board. The player with the white pieces always makes the first move, which is considered a small advantage.

The chessboard can also be described using coordinates. Files are labeled from a to h, and ranks are numbered from 1 to 8.

Using coordinates helps players describe moves, learn openings, and understand chess diagrams more easily.

Pieces at the Start

Each player begins with 16 pieces: 1 king, 1 queen, 2 rooks, 2 bishops, 2 knights, and 8 pawns.

Pieces have different strengths. In general, the queen is the strongest, pawns are the weakest, but pawns can become very powerful later in the game.

  • King: most important piece (if it gets checkmated, you lose)
  • Queen: strongest attacker and defender
  • Rooks/Bishops/Knights: main fighting pieces
  • Pawns: small steps, big impact

Pawn

The pawn is the smallest and most common piece in chess. It moves forward one square, but captures diagonally. On its very first move, a pawn may move two squares forward.

Pawns cannot move backward, which means every pawn move is important. Even though pawns seem weak, they play a huge role in controlling space and protecting other pieces.

If a pawn reaches the last rank on the board, it can be promoted into another piece, usually a queen. This makes pawns very dangerous in the endgame.

Knight

The knight moves in an L-shape: two squares in one direction and then one square to the side. It is the only piece that can jump over other pieces.

Knights are very useful in crowded positions where many pieces block the board. They can attack pieces that seem far away and create unexpected threats.

Because the knight’s move is unique, beginners should practice it often to avoid mistakes.

Bishop

The bishop moves diagonally across the board and can travel many squares in one move. Each bishop always stays on the same color square for the entire game.

Bishops are strong when the board is open and long diagonals are available. They are especially powerful when working together as a pair.

Good bishop placement can put pressure on the opponent and control important squares.

Rook

The rook moves in straight lines: up, down, left, and right. It is a very strong piece, especially later in the game.

Rooks work best on open files, where no pawns are blocking their path. They are often used to control the board and attack important targets.

Two rooks working together are very powerful and can easily dominate open positions.

Queen

The queen is the strongest piece on the board. She can move like a rook and a bishop combined.

Because the queen is so powerful, losing her early can be a big problem. Beginners should be careful not to move the queen too early in the game.

The queen is excellent for attacking, defending, and finishing games.

King

The king is the most important piece in chess. It moves one square in any direction.

Even though the king is not very strong early in the game, protecting it is the main goal. If the king is checkmated, the game is over.

In the endgame, the king becomes an active piece and helps support pawns and control important squares.

Quick Beginner Tips

Here are a few simple tips that help beginners play better:

  • Try to control the center (middle squares).
  • Develop your pieces: knights and bishops before moving the same piece many times.
  • Keep your king safe (castle when you learn it).
  • Don’t hang pieces: always check if a piece is defended.
